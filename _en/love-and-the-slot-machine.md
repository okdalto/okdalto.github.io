In English, a slot machine used to be called a "one-armed bandit." The single lever on its side looked like an arm, and because it kept taking your money it was a thief — a bandit. So a multi-armed bandit means a thief with many arms, or a situation with several slot machines lined up. The player has to choose one arm at every moment, without knowing which one will pay out the most.

The problem is simple. There are several slot machines. Each pays out with a different probability. But we don't know which one is good. We can pull only one lever at a time, and we see the reward that follows. The goal is to figure out which machine is best while, in the very process of figuring it out, collecting as much reward as possible.

This is where the difficulty arises. Should you keep pulling the machine you don't know well, or stick with the one that has looked best so far? The former is called exploration, the latter exploitation. Without exploration you never discover the better option. Without exploitation you never reap enough from the good option you've already found. The multi-armed bandit problem is, in the end, about how to balance exploration and exploitation in an uncertain world.

Love resembles this. We don't know in advance whom we should love, or which relationship will make us happy for a long time. Some people give you a strong rush from the start, but it doesn't last. Others are quiet at first, but grow steadier over time. Some relationships offer a big reward with wild variance; others look like a small reward but stay constant.

The early days of dating call for exploration. You meet different people, express yourself in different ways, and learn what draws you in and what wears you out. But exploration alone never deepens a relationship. At some point you have to spend your time on a single possibility, choose it again and again, and chip away at the uncertainty. Exploitation in love isn't merely settling for someone familiar — it's deepening a relationship once you've found one that's good enough.

And yet, as everyone knows, love is not a slot machine. A slot machine feels nothing. But the object of your love is not something to be chosen — it is someone who chooses, too. While I observe the other person, they observe me. When I change my choice, their response changes as well. So love is not the problem of picking a machine with a fixed reward distribution. It's closer to the problem of changing each other's reward distributions as you spend time together.

Even so, the multi-armed bandit offers one insight about love. To make the perfect choice you need information, and information is never given for free. To know someone, you have to spend time and emotion. But if you only ever keep investigating, you never choose anything. Conversely, if you choose too quickly, you may miss a better possibility.

Love is not a matter of the ability to recognize the optimal person at a single glance. It's the problem of deciding, in an uncertain world, what to investigate further, what to give up, and when to stop and go deep. In the end, what matters is not verifying every possibility, but recognizing one that's good enough and staying with it, responsibly.
